Injury and Pathology

Fractures

Types of fractures

  • Open (Compound): The broken bone pierces through the skin, increasing the risk of infection.
  • Closed (Simple): The bone is broken but does not penetrate the skin.
  • Simple: A clean break with minimal damage to surrounding tissues.
  • Comminuted: The bone shatters into multiple fragments, often requiring surgical intervention.

Healing stages for fractures:

Hematoma Formation (Days 1 - 7):

Blood vessels rupture, forming a clot (hematoma) at the injury site.
Inflammatory cells migrate to clean debris and promote healing.

Purpose:

  • Stabilizes the fracture temporarily.
  • Inflammatory cells (e.g., macrophages and neutrophils) clean debris and release signals to attract repair cells.

Callus Formation (Weeks 1 - 4):

Fibroblasts and chondrocytes form a soft callus made of cartilage and fibrous tissue.
Later, osteoblasts replace the soft callus with a hard, bony callus.

Purpose: Provides stability and prepares for bone remodeling.

Remodeling (Months to Years):

Osteoclasts resorb excess bone, reshaping it to restore normal structure and function.
Bone regains strength and density over time.

Sprains:

  • Injury involving ligaments, which connect bones at a joint.
  • Typically caused by stretching or tearing due to sudden twisting or impact.
  • Common locations: ankles, wrists, and knees.

Strain:

  • Injury involving muscles or tendons, which attach muscles to bones.
  • Often caused by overstretching or excessive force.
  • Common locations: lower back and hamstrings.

Arthritis

Osteoarthritis (OA):

  • Cause: Gradual breakdown of cartilage in weight-bearing joints such as knees, hips, and spine.
  • Symptoms: Pain, stiffness, and reduced joint flexibility.
  • Mechanism: Cartilage wears down, causing bones to rub against each other, leading to pain and bone spurs.

Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA):

  • Cause: Autoimmune disorder where the immune system attacks the synovial membrane (lining of joints).
  • Symptoms: Swelling, warmth, redness, and joint deformity.
  • Mechanism: Inflammation thickens the synovium, damaging cartilage and bones over time.
